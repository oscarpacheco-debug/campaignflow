The full PR campaign flow
From spotting an idea to a published, tracked backlink
Every good campaign starts here. A strong idea isn't just "an interesting fact" - it has to clear a few bars before it's worth your time. This phase decides whether everything downstream is easy or a struggle.
1What makes an idea worth pursuing
💬There's already journalist demand - the topic is being discussed
📊We can calculate something new - a unique number not widely available
🎯Backed by official sources we can rely on
⏱️Good timing - the moment maximizes impact
💰Has economic impact - energy journalists are usually economy journalists too
2Where to find ideas
📖 The Whale calendar (Trello)
Review the Whale Project's Trello board. Some ideas there are too technical for a standard Whale article but can be reshaped into a PR angle - they may need real calculation time and are worth more as Digital PR than as a quick article.
🧠 LLM and Google validation
Ask an LLM about domestic consumption patterns, trends, or lifestyle ideas related to energy. Then validate with a direct Google search - "is it true that...?" - to confirm the claim is accurate and genuinely newsworthy before building anything on top of it.
📊 Dedicated sources sheet
Keep a maintained sheet of official data sources: who publishes what, and how often they update. This is what lets you move fast when an idea needs a number today, not next week.

Once you have an idea worth pursuing, the report turns raw data into something a journalist can act on in minutes, not hours.
1
Choose the topicCurrent, useful, underexplored. Use Statista, Google News, IDAE/OCU/Eurostat/MITECO/REE for inspiration and validation.
2
Gather your sourcesOfficial statistics (kWh, CO2, euros), behavioral data, media context, studies - keep it digestible.
3
Draft with GPTBrief it with your sources and goals. Iterate with follow-ups: "make this more quotable", "add a real-world comparison", "explain your assumptions". Treat the first draft as a base, not a final version.
4
Add visuals and brandingpapernest brand style on headers, Flourish for charts, Canva for infographics and quote blocks.
✓Quality bar before moving to phase 3
A report is ready when its top 3 findings could each become a headline for three different outlets on their own. If you need three extra sentences of context before a number means anything, go back and sharpen the angle - see the Data Storytelling training for the full method.
The right journalist for the wrong report gets ignored. The right report to the wrong journalist also gets ignored. This phase is about matching the two precisely.

📋Step 2 - Execution mode reference
👑
Exclusive
Control the story - be the first and only source
High priority
Use when: we have unique data, a ranking, or an original study that nobody else has, with strong headline potential. No rush to be first to talk about the topic in general - we set the timing.
1
Teaser, not dataFirst contact is narrative and interest only - never reveal exact numbers yet. Keep asking interest, not giving the story away.
2
First batch - 15 to 20 contactsOne per journal, usually top-tier media (DR > 70). Only those who respond get the full PDF + charts + executive summary.
3
If interested, hold the lineStop outreach to others and wait to see if they want exclusivity - tell them about the study and offer to point to our site if they publish first.
4
Follow-up after 2-3 daysIf no one answered, launch a new batch at mid-tier (DR 50-70) of 15-20 journalists - this is the cascade. Try another batch if needed.
5
If nobody publishesPublish the study on our own domain and finish the process - the data still has SEO value even without press pickup.
Example: an original study on renewable consumption by region, or a ranking of the cheapest gas tariffs - something genuinely ours first.
Never reveal the full dataset in the first contact. The exclusivity only works if there's still something to offer once they say yes.
📊 About the cascade ("waterfall")
Cascade isn't a separate strategy - it's how you execute Exclusive when the first tier doesn't bite. You don't have to start at top-tier either: depending on the story, you can start the cascade at mid-tier and only escalate to top-tier if it gains traction, or start top and step down. The defining feature is moving progressively between tiers, not which tier you start at.
⚡
Newsjacking
Speed - enter a news cycle that's already happening
High priority
Use when: an external event is already public (TUR, ARERA, gas price, regulatory change) and we can calculate our own number from it within hours.
1
Blog published FIRST - mandatoryNo PDF, no exclusivity. The published page is what you link to.
2
2-3 key insights extractedThe number that matters to a household, stated plainly.
3
Email with 3-4 bullets and a linkNo attachments. Journalist list of 20-40 contacts, sent within hours to 1-2 days of the event.
4
Follow-up only with new dataIf the story develops further, update with something new - never "just checking in".
Example: TUR price is published by the regulator - same day, we publish our own simulation of what it means for an average household's bill.
💬
Mini-newsjacking
Insert into a conversation others already started
Not a current priority
Use when: the news is already covered by others and we have a complementary data point to add - not our own original analysis.
1
Identify the add-on insightA comparison or extra number that wasn't in the original coverage.
2
Quick blog or LinkedIn postNo heavy assets, no PDF.
3
Reach out only if askedIf a journalist who already covered the story asks for more, that's when you send.
Currently de-prioritized while the team focuses on the larger data models (PRVG, TRVE, AC reports). Know the pattern, but don't actively chase this one right now.
🌱
Blog / evergreen
No urgency - authority and SEO, not press coverage
High priority
Use when: content is useful but has no time pressure - a guide, historical evolution, or simulation by consumption profile.
Structured post with clear explanation and charts. No outreach required - distribute via our own domain, LinkedIn, and newsletter. The goal is branding and organic traffic, not a press hit.
Example: "How to calculate your gas bill" or a historical chart of TUR/PVPC evolution over the years.
🌡️
Barometer / tracker
A living data source, occasionally turned into a one-pager
High priority
Use when: we maintain a recurring tracker (like the Spain Energy Thermometer) and a fresh data point inside it becomes newsworthy on its own.
The barometer itself isn't a campaign - it's the engine. When an update inside it produces something strong enough (a new high, a sharp month-over-month move), that single data point gets pulled out and treated as its own mini-Exclusive or Newsjacking moment, with a one-pager built specifically around it.
Example: the energy thermometer updates monthly; the month PVPC hits a new yearly high, that becomes a standalone one-pager sent to a small batch of journalists.
⏲Cadence between sends
🛑
Don't release deep reports back to backIt saturates papernest's image with journalists.
📅
Minimum 2 weeks between major exclusivesUnless the second one is genuinely different and relevant.
⚡
Newsjacking is the exceptionYou can newsjack even inside that 2-week gap, since it's a different format - but don't follow it immediately with another deep report.
🎯
Every send needs a new or urgent angleThe goal is constant presence without ever looking like spam.
✓Final quality filter, before you send anything
Do not send if
❌ No strong insight
❌ No news hook or urgency
❌ No clear, specific data point
❌ It's pure commentary with nothing new
Send only if
✅ There is data
✅ There is a news angle
✅ There is meaning - someone would care
